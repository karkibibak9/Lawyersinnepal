Divorce is often a challenging and emotional process. In Nepal, the legal framework governing divorce and family matters has undergone significant changes with the implementation of the **Muluki Civil Code, 2074 (2017)**. This guide provides an overview of what you need to know if you are considering or undergoing a divorce in Nepal.

## Grounds for Divorce

Under the current law, both husbands and wives have the right to file for divorce. The grounds include:

- **Mutual Consent:** Husbands and wives can file for divorce at any time if they both agree to end the marriage.
- **Physical or Mental Torture:** If one spouse inflicts physical or mental suffering on the other.
- **Desertion:** If one spouse abandons the other for more than three years without consent.
- **Adultery:** If it is proven that a spouse has an extramarital relationship.
- **Incurable Disease:** In certain cases of contagious or incurable mental/physical diseases (though this is strictly interpreted).

## The Legal Process

1. **Filing the Petition:** The process begins by filing a petition in the District Court where either the husband or wife resides.
2. **Mediation Phase:** The court mandatory requires a mediation period. The judge will attempt to reconcile the parties unless there are extreme circumstances like domestic violence.
3. **One-Year Waiting Period:** In cases where there is no mutual consent, the court often gives a one-year period for the couple to reconsider before finalizing the divorce.
4. **Final Judgment:** If reconciliation fails, the court proceeds to a final hearing to decide on alimony, property division, and custody.

## Property Division and Alimony

Nepal's law follows the principle of equal distribution of "joint property" earned during the marriage. 

- **Share of Property:** A wife is entitled to a share of the husband's ancestral and earned property upon divorce, unless she opts for a lump-sum payment or monthly alimony.
- **Alimony:** If the wife does not have sufficient income, the court may order the husband to pay monthly alimony until she remarries.

## Child Custody and Support

The "Best Interest of the Child" is the primary factor in custody cases:

- **Under 5 Years:** Usually, the mother gets custody unless there are safety concerns.
- **Between 5 and 10 Years:** Preference is often given to the mother, but the father's involvement is encouraged.
- **Above 10 Years:** The court may take the child's preference into account.
- **Support:** Both parents are legally obligated to contribute to the child's education, healthcare, and upbringing expenses based on their respective incomes.
