Being accused of a criminal offense is a serious matter that can have life-altering consequences. In Nepal, the **Muluki Criminal Code, 2074** serves as the primary legislation governing crimes and punishments. Knowing your fundamental rights when dealing with the police and courts in Kathmandu is crucial for a fair defense.

## 1. Fundamental Rights of the Accused

The Constitution of Nepal guarantees several rights to individuals undergoing criminal investigation:
- **Right against Self-Incrimination:** You cannot be forced to testify against yourself or confess.
- **Right to Counsel:** You have the right to consult a legal practitioner of your choice from the moment of arrest.
- **Right to Information:** The police must inform you of the grounds for your arrest immediately.
- **24-Hour Rule:** Any person arrested must be produced before a judicial authority within 24 hours of arrest (excluding travel time).

## 2. The Arrest and Investigation Process

1. **First Information Report (FIR):** Most criminal cases begin with the registration of an FIR at a local police station.
2. **Police Custody:** The police may keep a suspect in custody for investigation only after obtaining a permit from the court.
3. **Statement Recording:** Any statement made before the police is generally not admissible as evidence in court unless it's a voluntary confession before a judge.

## 3. Bail Procedure in Nepal

Obtaining bail is a critical priority in criminal defense. The court decides on bail based on:
- The gravity of the offense.
- The likelihood of the accused tampering with evidence.
- The risk of the accused fleeing.

For minor offenses, bail is often granted through a security deposit or a personal guarantee. For more serious "Schedule 1" crimes, bail may be denied until the trial concludes.
